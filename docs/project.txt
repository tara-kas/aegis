Architecting Aegis: An Optimal MedTech Robotics Paradigm for Hackathon Victory
Abstract: The Aegis Concept
We are building "Aegis," an autonomous AI surgical monitoring and clinical workflow agent. At its core, it acts as a digital assistant in the operating room, using Google's HAI-DEF suite (specifically MedSigLIP) to analyse pre-operative medical imaging and real-time robotic telemetry simulated through Webots. To maintain a sterile environment, it utilises ElevenLabs' Scribe v2 for hands-free, hallucination-free medical speech-to-text, actively listening to the surgeon to automatically generate and format clinical notes into strict FHIR standards to replace legacy hospital databases.
Beyond clinical assistance, Aegis operates as an independent economic actor. It utilises the new Stripe Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP) and Shared Payment Tokens to autonomously lease computational power and manage its own financial transactions without exposing underlying credentials. It also leverages the Solana blockchain for sub-cent, high-frequency API micropayments, using Confidential Transfers to mathematically obscure transaction amounts and maintain strict hospital privacy. The entire workflow's profitability and outcome-based billing to the hospital are actively tracked using Paid.ai's agentic telemetry.
Finally, because medical AI operates in a high-stakes environment, the system is designed for absolute reliability and European regulatory compliance. The heavy cognitive reasoning is offloaded to massive models like DeepSeek-R1 hosted on Crusoe Cloud's ultra-low latency infrastructure. To comply with the EU AI Act and DORA regulations for high-risk medical devices, the agent's pipelines and data are governed by Red Hat OpenShift AI, while incident.io adaptive agents continuously monitor the system to automatically triage and mitigate any robotic kinematic anomalies or API latencies.

Strategic Alignment with Hackathon Challenges and Sponsor Tracks
To maximise the probability of success, a project must not merely utilise sponsor technologies; it must directly solve the core business challenges those sponsors have articulated. The Aegis architecture is explicitly designed to conquer multiple concurrent hackathon tracks by weaving their specific problem statements into a unified product narrative.
The first critical alignment is with the Avelios Medical challenge, which focuses on the impending obsolescence of legacy hospital information systems, specifically the SAP IS-H and i.s.h.med platforms, which are scheduled for discontinuation by 2030 and 2035, respectively.1 Avelios requires a replacement architecture capable of automatically generating up to two thousand unique, structured, and directly usable data points per patient treatment to fuel artificial intelligence and clinical research.1 Aegis addresses this directly by implementing a strictly Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) compliant data layer, transforming unstructured surgical telemetry and vocal clinical notes into deeply structured, semantically annotated data points ready for institutional use.1
Concurrently, the project addresses the BearingPoint Autonomous Consulting Agent challenge.3 While the specific instructions for this challenge require navigating digital transformation, Aegis embeds an autonomous consulting logic layer within its architecture. This sub-agent continuously analyses the hospital's robotic asset utilisation and billing efficiency, acting as a real-time autonomous consultant that recommends workflow optimisations and cost-saving measures, directly fulfilling the mandate for AI-driven enterprise advisory tools.3
Furthermore, the architecture integrates the incident.io Adaptable Agent challenge.5 In the life-critical domain of robotic surgery, system reliability must be absolute. Aegis deploys adaptive reliability agents, specifically the Service Level Agreement (SLA) Guardian and the Incident Commander, to automate triage and mitigation workflows if the robotic simulator detects kinematic anomalies or data latency.7 This directly answers the challenge to build AI that reconsiders its approach based on new information and maintains extreme accuracy in high-stakes environments.5
Finally, the project aggressively targets the autonomous monetisation tracks sponsored by Paid.ai, Stripe, and Solana.9 Traditional software-as-a-service billing models fail when applied to autonomous agents.9 Aegis proves its commercial viability by implementing the Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP) and Solana-based confidential micropayments, effectively allowing the surgical robot to lease its own computational resources, bill for specific clinical outcomes, and settle transactions without human intervention.11 This comprehensive alignment ensures that Aegis is not evaluated as a standalone prototype, but as a holistic enterprise solution spanning multiple sponsor domains.
Accelerated Genesis via Model Context Protocol and Vibe Coding
The most significant bottleneck in rapid software development, particularly within the severe time constraints of a hackathon, is the translation of abstract product requirements and architectural diagrams into functional, secure, and scalable code. To circumvent this friction, the Aegis architecture employs a highly optimised "Vibe Coding" pipeline utilising the Miro Model Context Protocol (MCP) Server, the Lovable.dev artificial intelligence coding agent, and Supabase's managed backend infrastructure.14
The development lifecycle commences within a Miro digital workspace, which serves as the immutable single source of truth for the entire engineering team. The board is populated with the Product Requirements Document (PRD), Unified Modelling Language (UML) sequence diagrams detailing the robotic data flow, entity-relationship diagrams for the FHIR database schema, and high-fidelity user interface mockups.16 By structuring the board with distinct, logically separated frames, the AI can parse the environment with extreme precision.16
The Miro MCP server acts as the critical bridge between this visual workspace and the integrated development environment (IDE). It exposes several highly specialised tools to the AI coding assistant, such as Claude Code or Cursor. The context_explore tool discovers high-level items like frames, documents, and prototypes, while the context_get tool retrieves specific text context, including HTML for documents and UI markup for prototypes.16 Additionally, the board_list_items tool assists the AI in navigating the spatial relationships between different architectural components on the board.16
When the development team initiates the coding process, they invoke the primary code_create_from_board prompt within their IDE.16 This prompt triggers a sophisticated two-phase execution plan. In the first phase, the AI agent calls context_get_board_context and items_list_items to deeply analyse the Miro board, summarising the text, diagrams, and documents to formulate a comprehensive understanding of the MedTech requirements.16 It subsequently presents a development plan to the engineers for confirmation. In the second phase, the AI generates specific documentation, implementation guidance, and the actual application files directly within the IDE.16
To translate this generated codebase into a live application, the MCP context is passed to Lovable.dev. Lovable is a platform explicitly designed for artificial intelligence-assisted development, automating both user interface generation and backend provisioning.18 Lovable features a native integration with Supabase, a platform that provides a fully managed PostgreSQL database, integrated authentication, Edge Functions, and real-time application programming interfaces (APIs).15
When the AI processes a prompt to create the clinician dashboard based on the Miro FHIR diagrams, Lovable automatically generates the React-based frontend components and concurrently configures the corresponding Supabase PostgreSQL tables.20 Crucially, this setup includes the automatic implementation of Row Level Security (RLS) policies, which are mandatory for maintaining patient data privacy and complying with enterprise security standards.15 This seamless end-to-end generation pipeline transforms abstract visual diagrams into a functional, secure application within minutes, allowing the engineering team to dedicate the vast majority of the hackathon duration to complex robotics integration and advanced clinical reasoning.19
Core Medical Intelligence and Foundational Interoperability
The clinical reasoning and diagnostic engine of Aegis is powered exclusively by the Google Health AI Developer Foundations (HAI-DEF) suite. The HAI-DEF suite represents a monumental leap in healthcare artificial intelligence, providing a repository of pre-trained, open-weight models that are specialised for medical domains.21 These models provide domain-specific embeddings that facilitate rapid AI development with significantly less labelled data, shorter training times, and reduced computational costs compared to traditional, generalised foundational models.22
A critical component of modern robotic-assisted surgery is the rapid, accurate pre-operative and intra-operative analysis of medical imaging. Aegis leverages MedSigLIP, a highly specialised accelerator within the HAI-DEF suite designed specifically for medical image analysis.21 MedSigLIP is a fine-tuned iteration of the SigLIP architecture, trained extensively on diverse medical modalities including chest X-rays, computed tomography (CT) slices, magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) slices, dermatology images, and histopathology patches.21
In the operational workflow of Aegis, simulated pre-operative Digital Imaging and Communications in Medicine (DICOM) files are processed through MedSigLIP. The model performs data-efficient zero-shot classification and semantic image retrieval to identify anatomical landmarks and potential pathological anomalies before the robotic procedure commences.21 Because the HAI-DEF model weights are entirely open, they can be downloaded and run locally or within a private cloud environment.21 This is a profound architectural advantage, as it ensures strict data sovereignty and compliance with institutional boundaries, a non-negotiable requirement for European medical facilities handling highly sensitive patient data.24 Furthermore, HAI-DEF features tight native integration with Google Cloud Platform (GCP), specifically Vertex AI and the Cloud Healthcare API, enabling developers to take these models from local research prototyping directly to scalable production services seamlessly.21
To effectively demonstrate the system's capabilities during a hackathon without violating the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) or the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA), Aegis must strictly avoid the use of real Protected Health Information (PHI). Instead, the architecture relies on deeply complex synthetic datasets mapped to the Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) standard.2 FHIR represents the global standard for health data interoperability, moving away from legacy message-based systems like HL7 v2 toward structured, reusable data objects exposed through modern APIs.2
The implementation focuses on the three foundational FHIR resources: Patient, Encounter, and Observation. The Patient resource acts as the absolute identity anchor, handling demographics and medical record numbers; without a perfectly structured Patient resource, all subsequent clinical data loses its vital context, compromising clinical safety.2 The Encounter resource defines the context of care, tracking inpatient versus outpatient statuses, timestamps, and locations.2 Finally, the Observation resource handles the actual clinical data points, such as telemetry from the surgical robot or diagnostic outputs from the MedSigLIP model.2
To populate the Supabase backend with this FHIR-compliant data, the engineering team utilises Synthea, an open-source synthetic patient generator.26 Synthea employs a Generic Module Framework to simulate the entire lifespan of a patient, modelling the progression and treatment of diseases based on real-world statistics collected by the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and the National Institutes of Health.26 The generator outputs highly realistic, yet entirely synthetic, FHIR resources, simulated DICOM images, physiological data, and clinical notes.26
For the specific context of intensive care and robotic procedures, this synthetic data is augmented with the MIMIC-IV dataset.30 MIMIC-IV provides a massive, de-identified repository of electronic health records sourced from the Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Centre, encompassing patient measurements, orders, procedures, and free-text clinical notes.31 By cross-referencing Synthea's structured FHIR output with the complex procedural realities found in MIMIC-IV, Aegis constructs a mathematically robust, privacy-compliant simulation environment that easily meets the Avelios challenge requirement of two thousand structured data points per treatment.1
High-Performance AI Infrastructure and LLM Orchestration
The deployment of complex, multimodal artificial intelligence agents capable of orchestrating surgical robotics requires underlying infrastructure that can handle massive parameter models with ultra-low latency and maximum throughput. Aegis relies entirely on Crusoe Cloud, a platform that provides a sustainable, carbon-neutral GPU computing environment specifically optimised for high-performance artificial intelligence workloads.33
Crusoe's managed AI services are centred around the Intelligence Foundry, which operates on a proprietary inference engine built with MemoryAlloy technology.35 Traditional inference engines suffer from severe latency bottlenecks because they must re-process the contextual prompt for every single query.37 MemoryAlloy solves this by acting as a cluster-native Key-Value (KV) cache fabric; it allows individual graphics processing units (GPUs) to share and fetch prefix caches across the entire cluster instantaneously.37 This hardware-software bridge maximises cache hits, delivering breakthrough speeds with up to a 9.9x acceleration in Time-To-First-Token (TTFT) and more than a fourfold gain in Queries Per Second (QPS).35 This extreme reduction in latency is the defining factor that allows Aegis to process robotic telemetry and clinical reasoning in real-time without introducing dangerous delays into the surgical workflow.
Aegis utilises Crusoe's OpenAI-compatible API endpoint to dynamically route different clinical reasoning tasks to the most appropriate frontier models available in the Intelligence Foundry.36 The selection of models is highly strategic, matching specific architectural requirements with the unique parameter strengths of individual large language models (LLMs). Kinematic Simulation and Vocal Telemetry
Demonstrating a functional MedTech robotics concept requires an exceptionally sophisticated physical simulation environment. The physical integration of actual robotic hardware is generally unfeasible within the logistical and temporal confines of a hackathon. Therefore, highly realistic physics simulators must be employed to generate the necessary kinematic data to validate the artificial intelligence algorithms.41
The architectural selection of the simulation framework fundamentally dictates the success of the robotics integration. The two primary candidates in the robotics community are Gazebo and Webots, each possessing distinct advantages. Gazebo is historically renowned for its deep, native integration with the Robot Operating System (ROS) and its capacity to handle large-scale, computationally intensive simulations.42 It utilises advanced rendering engines like OGRE v2 to deliver highly realistic lighting and shadows, and provides an exhaustive array of simulated sensors with customisable noise profiles.44
However, Gazebo possesses a remarkably steep learning curve, requiring profound familiarity with both the simulator's intricacies and the broader ROS ecosystem.42 In contrast, Webots represents the optimal choice for rapid prototyping and hackathon deployment.42 Webots excels in real-time simulations with moderate computational demands and features a contemporary, highly intuitive graphical user interface.42 Crucially, Webots allows developers to programme the robotic controllers directly in Python, C++, or Java without mandating a complex middleware layer, which aligns perfectly with the Python-centric AI models deployed on Crusoe Cloud.44

In the Aegis project, Webots is utilised to simulate a multi-axis robotic surgical arm, conceptually modelled after the Da Vinci surgical system.45 The Webots physics engine accurately simulates the kinematics of the surgical tool, calculating spatial coordinates, joint angles, and applied forces in real-time.44 This stream of raw kinematic data is transmitted continuously to the Supabase backend via Edge Functions, where it is formatted and forwarded to the Crusoe-hosted models for continuous anomaly detection.15 If the AI detects a movement trajectory that deviates from the established surgical norm, it immediately triggers an alert through the clinician dashboard.
In a sterile surgical environment, clinicians cannot utilise traditional keyboards or touchscreens to interact with the robotic agent; hands-free vocal communication is imperative. To achieve this, Aegis integrates ElevenLabs' voice artificial intelligence technology to serve as the primary human-computer interface.47 ElevenLabs provides advanced medical text-to-speech (TTS) systems that are specifically optimised for the healthcare industry.48 These models ensure that the pronunciation of complex pharmacological terms, anatomical references, and procedural instructions is executed with absolute clarity and empathetic tonality, fostering trust between the clinician and the system.47
More critically, for intra-operative commands and the generation of post-operative documentation, the architecture utilises the ElevenLabs Scribe v2 Speech-to-Text (STT) model.49 Standard, generalised STT models introduce severe, unacceptable risks in clinical settings. They are highly prone to hallucinating phrases, inserting false commentary during periods of silence, and failing to accurately process complex medical diction, particularly in noisy operating rooms.49 Scribe v2 was engineered specifically to eliminate these hallucinations and deliver extreme accuracy with sub-second latency.49 Furthermore, the model captures crucial non-verbal contextual cues, such as coughing or signs of distress, ensuring that the AI has a complete understanding of the patient's state.50 This flawlessly transcribed vocal data is immediately routed to the Crusoe-hosted DeepSeek model, which extracts the clinical entities and formats them directly into FHIR Observation resources, completely automating the tedious process of medical documentation and reducing administrative burnout.2
Autonomous Agentic Economics and Secure Settlement
A truly autonomous robotic system must not only possess the capability to execute physical and cognitive tasks but also the capacity to participate independently within the broader economic ecosystem. As artificial intelligence agents become increasingly sophisticated, they require the ability to negotiate, transact, and settle payments programmatically, in real-time, and without human intervention.11 Aegis incorporates a highly advanced Agentic Commerce layer to manage the leasing of simulated surgical equipment, the procurement of external computational APIs, and the execution of outcome-based billing to healthcare providers.
Traditional financial payment rails, which rely on multi-day clearing houses, credit card networks, and authorisation patterns that assume manual human confirmation, fundamentally break down when applied to high-frequency machine transactions operating at millisecond speeds.51 To solve this, Aegis implements the Agentic Commerce Protocol (ACP), an open-source specification co-developed by Stripe and OpenAI to enable seamless, programmatic commerce flows.12
Through the implementation of ACP, the Aegis robotic agent can dynamically purchase necessary resources on demand.12 The system is built upon the Stripe Shared Payment Token (SPT) architecture, which functions as a new payment primitive.52 When the agent initiates a transaction via a CreateCheckoutRequest, the buying institution's underlying payment credentials are never exposed.12 Instead, Stripe provisions an SPT that is strictly scoped to the specific merchant and the exact cart total.52 The agent passes this token back to the seller via the CompleteCheckoutRequest endpoint, allowing the business to process the transaction securely within their existing Stripe infrastructure while benefiting from enterprise-grade fraud protection.12
While Stripe handles macro-transactions, the architecture requires a different solution for micro-transactions—such as paying fractions of a cent per inference call on a language model or billing per second of robotic simulation time.11 For these continuous micro-streams, Aegis integrates the Solana blockchain ecosystem.11 Solana's sub-second transaction finality and negligible sub-cent fees make agent-to-agent micropayments economically viable at scale.11 This micro-economy is facilitated by the x402 protocol, an emerging standard that utilises the legacy HTTP 402 "Payment Required" status code.11 When the Aegis agent requests data from a paid API, the server responds with a 402 code and the payment terms. The agent automatically retries the request with a signed Solana transaction, which a facilitator settles on-chain, instantly unlocking the requested resource without requiring accounts or manual API keys.11
To maintain strict financial privacy—a paramount necessity in healthcare business-to-business transactions and regulatory environments—Aegis leverages Solana Token Extensions, specifically implementing the Confidential Transfer extension.13 Confidential transfers utilise highly advanced cryptographic techniques, specifically zero-knowledge proofs and homomorphic encryption, to mathematically obscure transaction amounts and account balances, while keeping the actual token account addresses public for verification.13
The technical implementation of this privacy layer requires the initialisation of a mint account utilising the ConfidentialTransferInstruction.13 The token accounts of the sender and receiver are manually configured by their owners to split balances into distinct "pending" and "available" states.56 When a transfer occurs, the tokens move to the pending balance, accompanied by cryptographic proofs that ensure the pre- and post-balances remain valid without revealing the actual numbers in plain text.55 Crucially, the protocol allows for the configuration of an auditor public key—specifically an ElGamal asymmetric public key—which empowers regulatory bodies or internal hospital auditors to decrypt the transaction amounts for compliance reporting without compromising the overall privacy of the network.13
The economic logic that governs when and how these transactions occur is managed by Paid.ai, an infrastructure platform specifically designed for AI agent monetisation.9 Traditional SaaS platforms rely on "per-seat" or "usage-based" metering, which fails to capture the true value delivered by an autonomous agent.9 Paid.ai provides ten critical features for agent billing, centred around outcome-based and signal-based tracking.9
Rather than billing the hospital purely based on the number of computational tokens consumed (which are merely implementation details), Paid.ai wraps the core artificial intelligence functions with telemetry trace calls.9 These traces measure actual business outcomes.9 For example, the Aegis robotic agent utilises a hybrid pricing framework: a predictable baseline subscription for deploying the "Surgical Assistant Agent" (the FTE Replacement Model), coupled with a per-workflow fee that triggers only when a diagnostic observation is successfully verified, formatted, and logged into the FHIR database (the Process Automation Model).9 Furthermore, Paid.ai tracks multi-vendor costs in real-time, monitoring the disparate expenses of ElevenLabs for voice synthesis, Crusoe for reasoning, and Google for image analysis.9 This real-time margin visibility ensures that the overall agentic workflow maintains profitable unit economics despite fluctuating underlying API costs, satisfying the core mandate of the Paid.ai hackathon challenge.9
Regulatory Governance and Operational Resilience
In the European Union, the deployment of artificial intelligence within the healthcare sector is governed by some of the most stringent and complex regulatory frameworks globally. A hackathon project that merely demonstrates technical feasibility will inevitably fall short; a project that seamlessly integrates compliance by design into its core architecture will unequivocally distinguish itself as an enterprise-ready solution.
The primary regulatory hurdle is navigating the interplay between the EU Artificial Intelligence Act (AI Act) and the Medical Device Regulation (MDR).59 The AI Act introduces a comprehensive risk classification system that applies directly to Medical Device AI (MDAI).59 AI systems utilised for medical purposes, such as the Aegis robotic assistant, are automatically classified as high-risk AI systems if they serve as a safety component of a medical device.59 Consequently, devices that fall under MDR classes IIa, IIb, and III will concurrently face this high-risk classification, subjecting manufacturers to a rigorous dual legal framework.59
Aegis is deliberately architected to address the exhaustive compliance requirements detailed in Annex VII of the AI Act.62 This mandates the generation of comprehensive technical documentation covering the system's architecture, monitoring protocols, performance metrics, and entire risk management lifecycle.62 Furthermore, because the system utilises powerful foundational models hosted on Crusoe Cloud, it must adhere to the obligations for Providers of General Purpose AI (GPAI) models with Systemic Risk.63 These obligations require continuous adversarial testing, systemic risk mitigation, and stringent cybersecurity protections.63 To ensure compliance, Aegis incorporates an autonomous compliance auditor agent that continuously monitors the system's outputs, ensuring adherence to the AI Act's transparency obligations—specifically verifying that clinical users are explicitly informed they are interacting with an AI system and that human oversight mechanisms remain untampered.62
Parallel to the AI Act is the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA), a critical regulation that mandates highly integrated digital entities and financial institutions implement measures to protect against cybersecurity threats and disruptions to Information and Communication Technology (ICT) services.64 Because Aegis orchestrates sensitive health data and handles financial settlements via agentic commerce, it falls under the purview of DORA's core pillars.64 The architecture fulfills these mandates by implementing continuous penetration testing protocols, mapping all third-party dependencies, and establishing highly structured incident reporting frameworks.64 Crucially, DORA requires the generation and continuous maintenance of Software Bill of Materials (SBOMs) for all open-source components, a process that is fully automated within the Aegis continuous integration pipeline.65
To operationalise this profound level of governance, Aegis relies on Red Hat OpenShift AI for comprehensive model lifecycle management.67 OpenShift AI provides an enterprise platform that inherently aligns with NIST 800-53 and PCI DSS controls through the implementation of isolated namespaces, role-based access control (RBAC), and encrypted etcd storage.67 The platform's Data Science Pipelines component is paramount to the architecture; it allows developers to visually construct machine learning workflows utilising Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs) powered by the KubeFlow Pipelines SDK.70
A DAG acts as the immutable blueprint for the entire process, mapping out every task from data preparation to model deployment, ensuring that operations only move forward and never circle back into infinite loops.70 This infrastructure guarantees that when the Google HAI-DEF models require retraining on new synthetic data, the process follows a strict, repeatable, and fully auditable pipeline.70 By providing a curated model registry with absolute traceability, OpenShift AI ensures that only validated, compliant models are ever deployed to the robotic endpoints in the clinical environment, whilst actively monitoring for model bias and data drift to maintain the extreme accuracy demanded by the MDR.69
Given the high-acuity, life-critical nature of robotic surgery, system reliability cannot be an afterthought. To address this, Aegis integrates highly advanced adaptive reliability agents powered by the incident.io platform.8 The incident.io ecosystem specialises in building reliable artificial intelligence for critical moments, treating AI as a fundamental component of system resilience rather than a mere operational overlay.71
Within the Aegis architecture, specific autonomous agents are deployed to safeguard the system.7 The SLA Guardian continuously manages compliance reporting, while the Error Budget Manager projects system usage against defined safety thresholds.7 Most importantly, the Incident Commander agent is tasked with automating mitigation workflows.7 If the Webots robotic simulator detects an anomaly in kinematic telemetry, or if the Crusoe API experiences latency, the Incident Commander does not act in dangerous isolation. Instead, it gathers all relevant diagnostic traces, summarises the failure state, and presents highly actionable remediation steps directly to the supervising clinical engineer via Slack.71 This intentional design choice ensures a "human in the loop" methodology, directly avoiding the documented "ironies of automation" where highly complex AI systems fail silently in high-stakes environments, thereby guaranteeing maximum patient safety and system reliability.71
